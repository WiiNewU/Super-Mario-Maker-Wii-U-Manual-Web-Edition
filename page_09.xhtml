10 Key Points

  • Decide on a Theme

    Maybe you want to make a course full of Goombas, or a zigzag course, or a course where Mario can bounce around on trampolines. With so many possibilities, it can help to decide what you want to focus on before you start building.

  • Only Use What You Need

    It's always tempting to pack as many things into the level as you can and watch the chaos unfold! But oddly enough, levels with too many elements aren't usually all that fun to play. With so much going on, the level just ends up looking like a bit of a mess, and it won't stick in players' minds for long. The really memorable levels are those that use a small number of elements to pull off something unique.

  • Really Focus

    It can be difficult to create a course while you've got other things on your mind. When you sit down to start building, try to avoid distractions. Free up some time by getting your chores and other priorities out of the way first, and then sit down and really go at it!

  • Create a Whole Course

    When creating a course, your first goal should be to get all the important pieces in place. Then play through the whole course from start to finish. Once you have a complete course to play through, you'll often notice all kinds of little things you missed while building it. This is because while you're concentrating on perfecting one part of the course, it can be hard to take in the bigger picture. Especially with longer courses, remember to play through the whole thing once in a while to get a feel for how it all fits together!

  • Be Open to Inspiration

    Whether it's something you saw while walking through town or something you heard while chatting with friends, the funniest little things can make you think, "Hey, what if I tried to do that in Super Mario Maker?" Whenever an idea comes to you, note it down! Even when you're not at your Wii U, coming up with ideas is all part of the fun!

  • Give Your Course a Name

    Whether you come up with it before you even start building, decide on it partway through, or leave it until the very end, it's important to give your course a good name. Legend has it that naming a course makes it come to life! ...OK, maybe that's pushing it, but still—it does give your course a sense of identity. Remember, the course name is the first thing a player will see when they encounter your course, so make sure it will get them wondering what might be in store!
    What's more, the name you came up with can actually help to inspire you as well. Say you called your course "Mountain Madness" but upon playing it realize it doesn't feel all that "mad." Doesn't that just give you the urge to throw in a few extra twists to make the course live up to its name?

  • Give Constructive Criticism

    When you play other makers' courses, don't just leave comments like "It was fun!" or "It was awful." Try to be a little more specific, saying things like "I loved the way this platform moved around!" or "That part with the Spiny was so cramped, it drove me crazy." Identifying which parts of a course you liked and which parts you disliked isn't just helpful for the course's maker—it can help you to improve as well! The more you think about what makes a course fun, the better your own courses will become!

  • Get a Second Opinion

    Once you've completed a course, upload it so that other people can play it. If you haven't quite finished making it yet, try showing it to your friends or family. You may have intended things one way, but other people will often see them quite differently. And by thinking about these different perspectives, you can come up with ways to convey your ideas more clearly to everyone.

  • In the End, It's Up to You!

    Listening to other people's feedback can help you notice things you might have missed, and they'll often give you good advice. But don't forget: this is your course, not theirs! In the end, it's up to you to decide what you want to do with your creation—the only right answer is the one that makes you happy.

Mastering the Craft

Facing the Unexpected
Remember, when a player tries your course for the first time, they have no idea what to expect. That's why they'll make mistakes and lose lives. But as they keep playing, they'll learn more about how the course works and they'll start to do better. The best Mario courses are those that make this learning experience as smooth and as satisfying as possible.
When playing a course you've created, you know everything in advance—the right way through the maze, where to find all the power-ups, where the next enemy is going to pop up—so you can dash through at top speed, doing only what needs to be done. You know exactly the route you wanted the player to take, so you can follow that route and make it look easy. And because it feels so easy, you might be tempted to make each step trickier to pull off, to give yourself a little more challenge.
But players facing your course for the first time won't have any of that knowledge. They'll get lost, miss mushrooms they should have grabbed, bump into enemies, and maybe even run out of time. If you're not careful, they'll get so frustrated that they'll give up before they ever reach the goal or discover that one satisfying route you wanted them to master.
That's why it's important to think about how a course is going to look to that first-time player who has no idea what to expect. You might want to give them room to learn and make mistakes without being too harshly punished, or you might want to carefully tweak your course to make it clearer which is the right way to proceed. The more you polish the experience, the more players will be able to enjoy your creation!
What Are You Doing?!
Say you've set up a clever mechanism in a course for the player to discover, but the player walks right past it without even paying attention. Why? Because the simple path seems easier, of course! Or perhaps you see a player who spends ages aimlessly jumping around in one section instead of heading for the goal. Why? Because they thought there might be a hidden secret to find!
The thing about players is, they never seem to approach your courses quite how you expect them to. They're going to play the game how they want to play it, whether that's how you designed it or not!
So what's the solution to this problem? One possibility is to make the intended route as obvious as possible, or you could block off all unintended paths so that your way is the only possible way to proceed. For tricky puzzles that the player is unlikely to figure out without some effort, this can be the best approach. But in other cases, you might want to take the opposite approach, keeping the course open and giving the player the freedom to tackle it in a variety of ways. After all, being able to jump about freely is one of the most appealing elements of the Mario series, and there's a lot of fun to be had in searching around for hidden secrets. Carefully balancing that freedom to explore with more-focused challenges to overcome is the basic philosophy of a standard Mario course.
At the end of the day, Super Mario Maker gives you the tools to create almost any kind of course you put your mind to—but it's always more satisfying when you hit on a design that lots of people can have fun with!
Drawing Pictures, Telling Stories
As you can see from the many tips above, there are lots of definite techniques that can be put to use when making courses. Many courses can be explained in terms of logic and reasoning.
But on the other hand, there are also courses that spring almost fully formed from a single random idea, or courses created just for the sake of painting a picture that came into your head. These types of courses are completely down to the individual creator's whims, and we can't begin to explain them here.
Just as some people are better at drawing beautiful pictures and others are better at telling funny stories, there's no single right or wrong way to go about making a course. It's up to each of us to experiment—so just try things out, and find the way that works best for you!
