The Soul of Super Mario Maker

I daresay many people have realized this already, but Super Mario Maker is something of a spiritual successor to a game called Mario Paint, released in 1992 for the SNES. It was a unique game where players could use a mouse to draw pictures and create music.

According to the staff who worked on that game all those years ago, they tried to throw in as many fun ideas as they could think of, even if they seemed a little silly. During development, there was a sort of banter between the staff members as they all created their own fun additions and tried to make each other laugh.

In Super Mario Maker, we've tried to take a similar approach, letting players do all kinds of things that would seem out of place in Mario's usual world. So if you want to put wings on everything, stack enemies into towers, give the enemies Super Mushrooms to make them grow instead of Mario, or even fill your stages with bizarre sound effects, that's all totally fine!

Of course we're also aiming to make a polished game that players can enjoy, but out of respect for the fun development process that gave birth to Mario Paint, we've packed in all kinds of little nods that players of that game might recognize. If you're not familiar with Mario Paint, you might find this game a little odd at times, but we're aiming to create a new and fresh kind of entertainment, where you can have fun creating all kinds of bizarre courses yourself and then enjoy watching your friends and family react to the results.

Cats' and Dogs' Paws

Did you know that, instead of the hand that normally appears on the TV screen while you're making a course, you can make a cat's paw or a dog's paw appear?
It's as simple as pressing the Right or Left Stick on the Wii U GamePad (depending on which hand you prefer!), but I'm sure a lot of people have never tried it out.

We actually had to go out and take photos of real cats and dogs for this feature, and it was a lot more work than we expected! Cats aren't exactly cooperative, and dogs are quite excitable...

None of our staff had done anything quite like these photo sessions before, so we were pretty worried about whether it would go all right. Just to be sure, we prepared two cats instead of just one. We started off photographing the white-furred cat, but it was always sticking its claws out, so we thought, "Oh dear, we're never going to be able to use these." Oddly enough, though, when we tried editing the pictures, they looked much better than we expected—so both cats' paws made it into the final game!

In the end, the photo session was a fun new experience for everyone. It probably helped that the animals we used were the pets of some of our team's members!

The Path to Sound Effects

The Sound Effects feature started out when we were wondering if we could work some kind of sound-based mode into the game, like the music-making feature in Mario Paint (released for SNES in 1992). In the end, it turned into a set of fun "toys" that can be used to liven up a stage in all kinds of unusual ways. Our own staff are sometimes surprised at the possibilities!

The fun part of this feature is that most of these sounds seem to have nothing to do with the game, so it's amusing to think of ways that they can actually fit into courses.

What's more, unlike Mario's usual sound effects, these ones are actually recorded using 3D audio technology, so they can sound surprisingly realistic.

When we were first testing the feature out, we had a set completely made out of weird sounds that seemed totally out of place in the game, but we got a lot of feedback saying people had no idea how to use them. In the end we mixed in a bunch of sounds that were a bit easier to find uses for. We ended up with about a 50-50 split.

You hear a lot of different sounds in your everyday life, but you never really get to think about which ones you'd like to hear in what situations, do you? I think it's pretty amazing to be able to set up these everyday sounds to play whenever and wherever you like.

The Path to Costume Mario

This feature actually started out when one of our staff members, who was working on the packaging and other artwork designs, suddenly came to us and said, "I made some pixel art of the amiibo characters. Do you think we can use them for anything?"

So we showed them to the designers on the development team, and they got really excited, saying, "We've got to use these somehow! How about we let people play as them?!" The first one we tried out was the Wii Fit Trainer, and when we saw it in motion, we all loved it.

In fact, everyone we showed this to was really enthusiastic, so we were all like, "Let's keep making them! Do you think we could make 30 or so?" And once we'd got started, the whole idea really picked up momentum until we ended up with a hundred of them!

Even after we had a whole set, though, we always used the Wii Fit Trainer to demonstrate the feature at internal presentations.

Programmer Talk

A:

They're asking us to tell some anecdotes from the programming side of development...

B:

Hmmmm, let's see...
We could talk about the sheer number of combinations? You know, how Mario can transform, then put on a shell for a helmet, then jump in a shoe with wings on it, then ride in the Koopa Clown Car all at the same time.

A:

You mean how difficult it was to program all these power-ups so that they could fit together in any combination?
Or how many checks needed to be run, once they were all working, to make sure they were all displayed properly and didn't cause any issues when bumping into things?

B:

That's right. The player might think, "Of course you can do stuff like that!" But I realize now how much effort it took to make all those things possible.

(Yikes, that does sound pretty difficult...)

A:

Yeah, but for people who can imagine all the fun situations the players can have as a result of that effort, it's easy to feel motivated to put in the hard work no matter how long it takes. So the team was having fun even as they were suffering.

(So the suffering was fun? That's what I call finding a silver lining!)

B:

We also often heard that we should just "make it feel right."

A:

Like they're leaving it up to you, right? Guess that means they think you're dependable, though.

B:

Dependable, huh? It sounds more relaxed and heartwarming to me...

A:

No, no... Instructions like "make it feel right" are sort of vague and easy to misunderstand. But if that's all they need to say, and the team members can visualize what that means, that's proof of how good the understanding is, right?
Also, there are a lot of people who've been involved in making Mario games at this point, and they share a certain understanding between them. It's relationships like those that really deepen the know-how of a development team.

(That's pretty deep! But I think I get what they're saying...)

B:

Oh, I see! Usually people talk about "know-how" as something that a single person builds up from their experience, and then they're encouraged to pass it on to other people by writing it down—but I guess it's possible for a whole team to build up a shared know-how too.
As a specific example of that, someone was saying how the programmers created the fly-swatting mode all on their own and were allowed to just "make it feel right," based on the minigame from Mario Paint.

A:

In the normal flow of a project, you'd have the planners giving an explanation to the programmers of exactly how they want something to look in the finished product, and they'd constantly be going back and having discussions about how the product was coming along. But in this case, all the planners had to say was, "Let the player swat flies, and make it feel right." So it was pretty much like... Planners: "Make this!" → Programmers: "Got it! ...Done!" → Planners: "Good job!"
...I guess that might be making it sound a little TOO simple...

B:

Apparently one of the programmers really did just say, "Fly swatting? You can leave that to me!"

A:

Then...my explanation might not be so far off the mark after all?

B:

By the way, are we really going to use a casual conversation like this for the programmers' feedback?

A:

Don't worry...I'll just ask the editor to "make it feel right!"

(Got it, "make it feel right." No pressure or anything!)
